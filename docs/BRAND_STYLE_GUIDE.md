# SA Church Finder Brand & Experience Guide

> A practical guide for keeping the product visually consistent, emotionally grounded, and aligned with the browse-first directory experience we just shaped.

## Purpose

SA Church Finder should feel like a trusted local guide for people looking for a spiritual home in San Antonio.

The experience is not meant to feel institutional, heavy, or overly church-administrative. It should feel:

- welcoming
- calm
- polished
- modern
- local
- trustworthy
- easy to browse without pressure

The app should give people the feeling that they can explore at their own pace, compare options naturally, and move from curiosity to confidence without friction.

## Core Story

This is a church discovery experience built with the ease of a modern travel or hospitality directory, but with the warmth and respect appropriate to spiritual decision-making.

The product story is:

People are not just finding a building. They are looking for belonging, rhythm, familiarity, and a place that feels right. The interface should support that search gently.

Every screen should reinforce these ideas:

- discovery should feel easy
- information should feel trustworthy
- exploration should feel safe
- choices should feel local and real
- the product should never feel pushy or gimmicky

## Brand Promise

SA Church Finder helps people discover churches in a way that feels clear, welcoming, and grounded in the real character of San Antonio.

## Experience Principles

### 1. Browse First

The product should invite exploration before commitment.

- users should be able to scan, compare, and map churches quickly
- the list and map should feel like equal parts of one experience
- filters should narrow choices without making the interface feel dense or technical

### 2. Warm, Not Loud

The interface should be confident and friendly, not flashy.

- use warmth through color, photography, and language
- avoid overdesigned religious symbolism or decorative clutter
- avoid marketing language that feels exaggerated or sales-heavy

### 3. Trust Through Clarity

The product earns trust by making information legible and predictable.

- church cards should surface the most decision-useful facts first
- the map should feel stable and readable
- filters and sort controls should be obvious, lightweight, and reversible

### 4. Local and Grounded

This should feel rooted in San Antonio, not like a generic national directory.

- mention neighborhoods naturally
- highlight denominations, mission history, and real-world context
- favor authentic church details over abstract platform language

### 5. Calm Momentum

Users should always feel like they know what to do next.

- the page should encourage forward movement without pressure
- use soft hierarchy and clear control groupings
- preserve momentum between search, map movement, and card browsing

## Personality

If the interface were a person, it would be:

- thoughtful
- hospitable
- informed
- optimistic
- respectful
- never preachy

It should feel more like a helpful host than a lead-generation funnel.

## Visual Direction

### Overall Look

The reference direction is a hospitality-style discovery UI:

- bright white canvas
- soft gray borders
- restrained shadows
- rounded controls
- clean card grids
- a strong but limited accent color

The interface should feel familiar to people who have used polished directory and booking products, while still reading as church-focused.

### Color Story

The palette should support warmth, trust, and readability.

Primary accent:

- coral / rose gradient for search and primary calls to action
- example: `#FF385C` with the gradient already used in the app

Core neutrals:

- white backgrounds
- charcoal text
- soft gray dividers
- subdued gray secondary copy

Support tones:

- deep navy for the hero banner
- pale green for service-status chips
- warm gray for supporting surfaces

Use color intentionally:

- coral means action or emphasis
- navy means depth, story, or featured content
- green means helpful status
- neutrals do most of the work

### Typography

Typography should feel direct, modern, and highly legible.

- use a clean sans-serif face
- favor strong weight contrast over decorative typography
- headings should feel crisp and confident
- body text should stay conversational and easy to scan

Text should read like a polished consumer product, not a church bulletin and not a corporate SaaS dashboard.

### Shape Language

Rounded geometry is an important part of the product feel.

- rounded search pill
- rounded control buttons
- rounded cards
- rounded map/list floating toggle

Rounded elements should create softness and friendliness, but still feel precise.

## Signature Interface Patterns

These patterns define the product and should remain stable unless there is a strong reason to change them.

### Header Search Pill

The header search bar is the product’s anchor.

It should communicate:

- location
- search intent
- time/filter intent
- one clear primary action

It should feel lightweight, high-trust, and central to the experience.

### Category Rail

The horizontal category rail should feel exploratory, not technical.

- it acts like a browsing invitation
- it should be visually airy and easy to scan
- icons should feel friendly and legible at a glance
- it should reward curiosity rather than demand precision

### Hero Banner

The hero banner is a story moment, not a generic marketing block.

Its job is to reassure the user that this is a thoughtful, city-specific discovery tool.

It should communicate:

- welcome
- breadth of choice
- confidence in the directory
- emotional permission to explore

### Church Cards

Cards are the main decision surface.

Each card should prioritize:

1. recognizability
2. trust signals
3. quick comparison
4. next action

Cards should feel easy to skim in batches. Dense cards or overly stylized cards break the experience.

### Floating Map/List Toggle

This control is a key part of the app’s rhythm.

It should feel:

- clear
- persistent
- mobile friendly
- one-tap obvious

It is not just a control. It is part of the product’s browse-first identity.

## Imagery

Photography should feel human, warm, and rooted in real church life.

Prefer:

- congregation moments
- architecture with character
- welcoming community scenes
- authentic light and texture

Avoid:

- generic stock imagery with no sense of place
- overly staged worship imagery
- highly saturated or overly dramatic treatments
- imagery that feels luxury-travel instead of community-centered

Fallback states should still feel polished and calm, never broken or empty.

## Motion

Motion should be subtle and supportive.

Good uses:

- hover lift on cards
- slight search button scale on interaction
- smooth modal and overlay transitions
- map/list state changes that preserve orientation

Avoid:

- novelty animation
- bouncy transitions
- attention-seeking micro-interactions
- movement that competes with reading or browsing

## Voice and Copy

Copy should sound:

- warm
- concise
- plainspoken
- encouraging
- respectful

The product should guide without sounding overly formal or overly clever.

### Good Copy Characteristics

- “Find your spiritual home in San Antonio”
- “Browse by style or denomination”
- “Show map”
- “Show list”
- “Guest favorite”

### Avoid

- buzzwords
- startup jargon
- overly promotional claims
- pushy conversion language
- heavy theological positioning

The interface should serve many kinds of church seekers. The tone should stay hospitable and broad.

## Interaction Rules

### Controls

- primary actions use the coral gradient or solid dark emphasis
- secondary actions are white with subtle borders
- filter actions should always feel reversible
- controls should stay understandable without tooltips

### Information Density

- show the most useful information first
- avoid stacking too many badges
- avoid large blocks of explanatory text in core browsing flows
- let layout and hierarchy do the work

### Mobile Behavior

On mobile, the experience should preserve the same identity as desktop rather than becoming a different product.

- keep the floating map/list toggle prominent
- preserve the search-first entry point
- make category browsing horizontal and tactile
- maintain generous tap targets and clear spacing

## Accessibility and Inclusivity

The brand should feel open and usable to a broad audience.

- maintain strong color contrast
- do not rely on color alone for meaning
- ensure controls are keyboard reachable
- keep language simple and welcoming
- support users who are unfamiliar with denominations or church terminology

The product should not assume deep prior knowledge.

## What the Product Should Never Become

Avoid drifting toward:

- a church management dashboard
- a coupon or deal site
- a generic real-estate listing clone
- an over-themed religious website
- a cluttered tourism guide

The app should stay in the space between consumer-grade polish and community-rooted warmth.

## Design Checklist

Before shipping UI changes, ask:

- Does this make browsing easier?
- Does this feel welcoming rather than transactional?
- Does this still feel rooted in San Antonio?
- Does this preserve the calm, polished directory feel?
- Does this make the church search feel more trustworthy?
- Does this introduce clutter or remove it?

If a change improves function but weakens the emotional tone, it should be reconsidered.

## North Star Summary

SA Church Finder should feel like the easiest, warmest, and most trustworthy way to explore churches in San Antonio.

It should combine:

- the usability of a top-tier directory
- the emotional softness of a hospitality product
- the grounded credibility of a local guide
- the respectfulness required for a meaningful life decision

That combination is the brand.
